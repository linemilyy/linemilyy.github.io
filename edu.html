Ed.gov Redesign

Overview

This project was done as part of our program, we were assigned to redesign a government website, revamping it to find solutions to the potential existing issues. My two team members and I redesigned the Ed.gov website, streamlining and updating icons to make the overall website more easily accessible to viewers.

Deliverables

  • - Redesigned Header Navigation
  • - Heuristic Evaluation
  • - Wireframes
  • - Final Prototype

    • Methods

    Interviews, Surveys, Affinity Mapping, Heuristic Evaluations, Color Testing, User Testing, Wireframing, Prototyping

    Tools

    Mural, Figma, Adobe XD, Adobe Illustrator, Zoom, Microsoft Powerpoint, Google Drive

    Initial Research

    To start off our brainstorm on how to redesign the Ed.gov webpage, we conducted a Heuristic Evaluation as a team, discussing with each other what we thought was most important to keep users engaged on our page.

    Heuristic Evaluation

    We then individually clicked through the website and discussed our thoughts and ideas on how we could revamp the webpage.

    User Research

    To start our research phase, my teammates and I came up with our main objective:

    Test users of varying ages and different restrictions (i.e. language barriers, sight restrictions, color blindness) to see how they interact with the website while maintaining unique motivations.

    Before our user tests, we created two proto personas to help us find the right demographic of people to base our research on.

    Proto Persona

    User Insight

    We asked our users to look for qualifying and relevant student loans and grants on the ed.gov website along with necessary contact information relating to these topics.

    Overall, the takeaways from our user test were generally negative. Most users were overwhelmed by the amount of information on each page and had difficulty navigating through the website due to the inability to differentiate between sections on the home page. To categorize the comments from our user tests, we created an affinity diagram and discussed the main pain points from our research.

    We decided to use our final takeaways to jumpstart our ideas on how to improve the overall user experience. These takeaways included:

  • - Overwhelming amount of infoformation on each page
  • - Lack of hierarchy throughout the website
  • - Difficulty understanding text presented on each page
  • - Multiple broken links
  • - Confussing categorization

    • Problem Statement

    How might we optimize the current ed.gov website to provide users with an informative yet simple experience measured by the time spent on the website and amount of useful information taken from their visit?

    Ideation

    As we knew a large part of the current issue of the Ed.gov webpage was the lack of hierarchy and organization, we started off our ideation process with simple web page layouts and discussions on the navigation bar. As we sorted through the existing information and categories offered on the website, we were able to define specified categories to sort the information under.

    As color and fonts are hand in hand with visual hierarchy, we also tested the colors on the current Ed.gov webpage to see how we could boost the visual hierarchy of the page.

    Color Testing

    To fix the failed color test, we changed the text color from #78AE63 to #000000 and #1860AC against the #DAECF7 background color.

    UI Branding

    We wanted to keep a very sophisticated and regal image for the Ed.gov webpage so we chose a color scheme based on some research on existing university logos and color palettes.

    As the navigation bar was an important emphasis we wanted to focus on in our redesign, we ran through a variety of potential options before our final decision.
